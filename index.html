Identifying Roof Material Start Here
1
EPDM rubber: White on top, black backing. Feels rubbery and stretchy — stretch it and it fully returns to shape. Matte finish. Most common on trailers and older Class A/C units.
2
TPO: White on both sides, sometimes fleece backing. Slightly more rigid than EPDM, semi-gloss finish. Stretch it and it does NOT fully return — leaves a slight wrinkle. Less slippery when wet.
3
Fiberglass: Hard, smooth, rigid surface. Common on higher-end motorhomes. Gel coat over fiberglass cloth. Can oxidize and lose shine over time.
4
Aluminum: Metal panels with visible seams and rivets. Rare on newer units — mostly older RVs. Heavy but durable.
5
Quick ID trick: Remove the interior trim ring around a roof vent or skylight and inspect the edge of the roof membrane. EPDM has a black backing, TPO is the same color on both sides.
⚠ NEVER use petroleum-based cleaners, citrus cleaners, or acetone on EPDM or TPO — causes permanent damage. Always confirm roof type before applying any product.
💡 Do NOT seal around AC units — this can block condensation drainage and push water inside. Seal around the base flange only.

Vent Inspection & Resealing
1
Inspect first: Check vent cover for cracks, warping, or UV damage. Inspect the flange where vent meets roof membrane for cracked, brittle, or missing sealant.
2
Remove old sealant: Use a plastic scraper only — metal tools can cut the membrane. Soften stubborn sealant with a heat gun on low setting held 10–12 inches away.
3
Apply butyl tape first: Place butyl tape over all screw holes around the vent flange before applying lap sealant. This seals the fastener penetrations.
4
Self-leveling sealant: Apply a continuous bead of Dicor self-leveling lap sealant completely around the vent flange. Smooth with a putty knife to eliminate air pockets.
5
Cure time: Allow 24–48 hours to fully cure before reinstalling vent cover. Test after next rain event to confirm no leaks.
⚠ Vent flanges are one of the most common leak points on any RV. Check and reseal annually minimum — more often in Maine's freeze/thaw cycles.
Vent Cover Replacement
1
Measure correctly: Standard vents are 14" x 14" but verify yours — some older units use 13" x 14". Measure the opening not the cover.
2
Replace the insert separately: The inner dome/insert can often be replaced without removing the outer shell. Check if only the dome is cracked before pulling the whole unit.
3
Full replacement: Remove all screws around perimeter — they are hidden under sealant. Once out, install new unit with fresh butyl tape under flange, then lap sealant over all screws and edges.
✓ Pro tip: Consider upgrading to a Maxxair or Fan-Tastic vent cover — the raised design dramatically reduces rain intrusion and is a good upsell for customers with chronic vent leaks.

Skylight Inspection & Reseal
1
Inspect from inside first: Remove interior trim ring and look for water staining, delamination, or damaged membrane around the opening edge.
2
Inspect from roof: Look for cracked or yellowed dome, gaps in sealant at all four corners, loose or missing screws. Corners are the highest-failure spots.
3
Reseal procedure: Use plastic scraper to remove all old sealant. Clean with mineral spirits then isopropyl alcohol. Allow to fully dry. Apply fresh self-leveling lap sealant completely around perimeter — no gaps.
4
Cover old screw holes: Run a bead of sealant over every old screw hole before positioning new skylight. Press skylight down — sealant should squeeze up through screw holes confirming a seal.
Skylight Replacement
1
Remove screws first: Use a screw gun — screws are tight and numerous. Remove sealant from around each screw head before attempting removal.
2
Clean the opening: Scrape all old sealant with a plastic putty knife. Use mineral spirits for residue — use sparingly on non-fiberglass roofs.
3
Match the size exactly: Skylights are not universal. Check manufacturer, measure opening, and order the correct replacement unit before removing old one.
4
Double-stick tape trick: Apply double-sided butyl tape to the flange of the new skylight before setting it in place. This acts as a primary seal under the sealant bead.
⚠ Cracked skylights are a common hidden leak source. A yellowed or crazed dome may look okay but let UV-degraded sealant fail silently. When in doubt replace the whole unit — domes are inexpensive.

Satellite Dish Mount — Leak Prevention & Repair
1
Mount feet are the leak point: Satellite dishes mount on multiple feet bolted through the roof. Each bolt hole is a potential entry point. Inspect all feet for sealant condition.
2
Reseal procedure: Remove any old cracked Dicor around each foot. Clean area with isopropyl alcohol. Apply fresh self-leveling Dicor around every foot and over every fastener head.
3
Cable entry points: The coax cable penetration through the roof is a separate leak source. Inspect and reseal the rubber boot or cable entry gland. Apply Dicor over the boot perimeter.
4
Removing a dome satellite: Open clamshell, unscrew from mounting feet, remove dome. Work mounting feet free gently. Remove old Dicor puddle, clean area, apply EternaBond patches over each mount location — 6" diameter minimum.
5
Patching abandoned holes: Fill screw holes with same-size flat-head stainless screws bedded in Dicor, then overlay with Dicor. Or use EternaBond patch over the entire area for a permanent seal.
💡 Starlinkand other flat antenna mounts are increasingly common. Same principle — inspect every mounting fastener and cable entry annually. Self-leveling Dicor on all fasteners after installation.

WiFi Hub / Cell Booster Mount
1
Common units: WeBoost, Winegard ConnecT, and similar cell boosters have roof-mounted antennas. Inspect all mounting screws and cable entry points for sealant failure.
2
Cable entry boot: Most WiFi/cell antennas use a rubber grommet or boot where the cable penetrates the roof. These are notorious leak points — inspect closely and reseal around the entire base with Dicor.

TV Antenna — Leak Diagnosis & Repair
1
Most common leak point: The crank-up batwing antenna (Winegard) has a shaft that goes through the roof to an interior crank handle. The shaft O-ring degrades and the base sealant cracks over time.
2
Check the O-ring first: Water dripping from the interior crank is often an O-ring failure — not a roof sealant issue. Remove the crank, inspect the O-ring on the shaft, and replace if dried/cracked.
3
Reseal the base: Clean existing sealant with mineral spirits then alcohol. Apply fresh Dicor self-leveling sealant around the entire antenna base perimeter. Generous application.
4
Rubber boot / coax entry: The coax cable entry has a rubber boot. These dry out and pull away from the base. Reseal around the boot with Dicor or use EternaBond for a long-term fix.
5
Antenna replacement: If removing old crank-up, consider upgrading to a Winegard Sensar or similar low-profile non-crank unit. Eliminates the shaft penetration entirely and gets better reception.
⚠ Do NOT use silicone on antenna bases. If silicone has been used previously, remove it completely before applying Dicor or EternaBond — nothing bonds to silicone residue.
💡 Lube the antenna mechanism annually with silicone spray — not WD-40. A dry mechanism causes users to force the crank which stresses the base seal.

Seam Inspection & Repair
1
Where seams fail most: Front and rear cap edges, where roof meets side walls, and anywhere the membrane laps over a roof edge or trim piece. Thermal expansion constantly works seams loose.
2
Remove all old sealant: Never apply new sealant over old cracked sealant — it will fail quickly. Use a plastic scraper to remove all old material. A heat gun on low helps soften stubborn sealant.
3
Clean thoroughly: Wipe down with mineral spirits then isopropyl alcohol. Allow to fully dry — any moisture under new sealant causes adhesion failure.
4
Self-leveling for flat seams: Apply Dicor self-leveling lap sealant on horizontal surfaces. It flows into the seam naturally and levels off. Apply a continuous bead — no breaks.
5
Non-sag for vertical seams: Use Dicor non-sag sealant on vertical surfaces and edges. Self-leveling sealant will run off vertical surfaces.
6
EternaBond for long-term: For permanent seam repairs, EternaBond tape over clean surface provides 20+ year bond. Clean with mineral spirits first — EternaBond will NOT stick to silicone residue.
⚠ TPO seams are heat-welded from the factory — standard lap sealant doesn't bond to TPO the same way as EPDM. Use TPO-compatible products and primer for TPO seam repairs.

Roof Cracks & Membrane Repair by Type
1
EPDM crack/tear repair: Clean with approved solvent, apply EPDM primer, cut patch 2 inches larger than damaged area on all sides. Apply compatible adhesive to patch and roof surface, wait until tacky, press patch firmly rolling out all air bubbles. Seal edges with self-leveling lap sealant.
2
TPO crack repair: Clean with isopropyl alcohol, apply TPO-specific primer, apply TPO patch tape or use heat gun to weld patch for factory-quality bond. Standard EPDM sealant will not properly bond to TPO.
3
Fiberglass crack repair: Sand edges around crack to remove gelcoat. Apply epoxy resin into crack, embed fiberglass cloth layer, apply additional resin layers, allow to cure, sand smooth, finish with matching gelcoat.
4
Hairline cracks: On EPDM and TPO — apply self-leveling lap sealant over hairline cracks as a first response. Monitor closely and patch properly if crack grows.
5
Soft spots: Soft or spongy areas indicate water has penetrated to the substrate. This is a bigger job — decking may be rotted and may require membrane removal and subfloor repair before re-roofing.
⛔ Soft spots or delamination need immediate attention — water damage progresses fast in RV roofs. Document with photos and advise customer in writing if they decline repair.
Leak Location Method
1
Start inside — find ceiling staining or wet spots. Note location relative to roof features above.
2
Remember: water travels. Entry point on roof may be several feet from where it shows inside. Work uphill from interior damage location.
3
Use garden hose test — have someone inside watching while you run water section by section on roof. Pinpoints entry zone.
4
Musty smell indicates existing moisture/mold — water has been entering for some time even if no visible staining yet.

Ladder Mount Leak Repair
1
Why ladders leak: Ladder mounts bolt through the rear cap or roof edge. Vibration from road travel constantly works the fasteners and surrounding sealant loose. Often overlooked during routine inspections.
2
Inspect all mount points: Check every ladder bracket fastener location — top and bottom of each side rail. Look for cracked or missing sealant, rust staining, or surface separation around mount plates.
3
Reseal procedure: Remove old sealant with plastic scraper and mineral spirits. Apply fresh butyl tape under any mount plate being resealed. Apply self-leveling Dicor around all fastener heads and mount plate edges.
4
Check fastener tightness: Loose mounting bolts accelerate sealant failure. Tighten all ladder mount hardware before resealing. Use stainless steel hardware as replacements if existing hardware is rusted.
5
EternaBond over mount plates: For a long-term solution, run EternaBond tape across the entire mount plate and several inches onto the surrounding roof material after resealing with Dicor.
💡 Ladder mount leaks often show up as water intrusion at the rear interior wall or ceiling near the back cap — customers may not connect the leak to the ladder until you show them.

DOT vs ASME Tanks — Know the Difference Critical
1
DOT cylinders are portable tanks (20 lb, 30 lb) on travel trailers and 5th wheels. Must be recertified 10 years from manufacture date stamped on collar, then every 5 years after. Format: month/year (e.g. 06/15 = June 2015).
2
ASME tanks are permanently mounted on motorhomes. Built with thicker steel — no recertification required. Visually inspect annually for damage. If a zigzag rust line appears on the tank shell — that is a crack — replace immediately.
3
DOT recertification periods: Visual inspection = 5 more years. Proof pressure test = 7 more years. Volumetric expansion test = 12 more years. Cost typically $35–$60 at a bulk propane distributor.
4
Automatic fail conditions: Dents, cracks, rust pitting, fire damage, missing foot ring, broken valve collar, leaking or defective OPD valve, missing date stamp.
⚠ Propane fill stations are required to inspect DOT tanks before filling. An expired or condemned tank cannot be legally refilled. Always check the date stamp before visiting a customer — expired tanks are a common service call.
Tank Inspection Procedure
1
Inspect exterior for dents, bulges, cracks, deep rust, and fire damage. Light surface rust is normal — heavy rust or pitting is a fail.
2
Check the protective ring around the service valve — bent or broken means the valve is unprotected. Check valve collar on ASME tanks for mounting hardware condition.
3
Inspect the bottom of DOT cylinders — sitting in the mounting tray collects moisture. This is the most common rust location.
4
Check OPD valve operation (the triangular handwheel) — should open and close smoothly. A stuck OPD is a common reason tanks are rejected at fill stations.
5
Inspect the regulator — replace every 10–15 years. Check rubber pigtail hoses for cracks, stiffness, and UV damage. Replace hoses every 5–10 years regardless of condition.
💡 Pro tip: Replace pigtail hoses in pairs. Sell the service to customers as a package — tank inspection + pigtail replacement + bubble test. High value, low parts cost, fast job.

Propane Leak Detection — Bubble Test Method
1
Safety first: Turn off all appliances before testing. Never use an open flame to detect propane leaks. Ventilate the area first.
2
Bubble test: Mix 50/50 dish soap and water in a spray bottle. With tank valve slowly opened and all appliances off, spray generously over all connections, fittings, hose joints, regulator, and valve connections. Any bubbles = leak found.
3
Regulator diaphragm test: Find the small vent hole on the regulator. Place finger over the hole and apply soapy water. Growing bubbles mean the diaphragm has failed — replace regulator.
4
Pressure drop test (NFPA 1192): Connect manometer to system. Charge to 11 inches water column (WC). Close tank valve. Bleed pressure down to 8 inches WC. Wait 3 minutes — pressure must not drop. Any drop indicates a leak. This is the official test standard.
5
Excess flow valve: If appliances won't light after tank connection, the excess flow valve may have tripped. Close tank valve, wait 5 minutes, reopen VERY SLOWLY — opens to fully open position. Never crack the valve quickly.
⛔ Never use regular Teflon tape on propane fittings — use only yellow gas-rated pipe thread sealant or yellow PTFE tape specifically marked for gas lines.
Regulator Troubleshooting
1
System pressure spec: Normal operating pressure is 11 inches WC. Acceptable range is 11–14 inches WC. Outside this range — inspect and adjust or replace regulator.
2
Humming regulator: Low-temperature operation can cause regulator diaphragm humming. Common in cold weather. Usually means regulator is aging — replacement recommended.
3
Yellow or orange flame: Indicates incomplete combustion — could be regulator pressure too low, dirty burner orifice, or insufficient air. Healthy propane flame is blue.
4
Burner pops when shutting off: Pressure slightly high or damaged burner. Check regulator output pressure first, then inspect individual burner.
💡 A manometer is essential for professional propane system work. A quality digital manometer runs $40–$80 and pays for itself on the first pressure drop test job.

Outdoor Receptacle Diagnosis
1
Most common cause: Exterior outlets are almost always protected by an interior GFCI outlet — usually in the bathroom or kitchen. Check and reset that GFCI first before doing anything else.
2
GFCI mapping: Multiple outlets can share one GFCI circuit. Use a outlet tester to map which outlets lose power when you trip each GFCI. Exterior outlets are frequently downstream of interior GFCIs.
3
If GFCI keeps tripping: Disconnect all devices from all protected outlets. If it holds — load was tripping it. If it still trips immediately — there is a fault in the wiring or a downstream outlet is defective.
4
Moisture is the #1 cause: Exterior outlets are exposed to rain and condensation. Moisture inside the outlet box causes nuisance tripping. Dry out completely, check cover gasket condition and replace if hardened or missing.
5
Outlet cover replacement: Always use an in-use weatherproof cover (bubble cover) on exterior outlets — allows cords to plug in while maintaining weather seal. Standard flip covers don't seal with a cord plugged in.
6
Replacing the outlet: Must use a weatherproof rated GFCI outlet for exterior locations. Standard indoor GFCI not rated for outdoor exposure. Turn off breaker and verify power is off with multimeter before opening box.
⚠ NEVER work on outlets with shore power connected. Always disconnect from campground pedestal and verify power is off at the outlet with a multimeter before touching any wiring.

Coach Light Diagnosis and Repair
1
12V DC system: Coach lights run on 12-volt DC power from the house battery through the converter. If all lights are out check the house battery charge and converter output first.
2
Check fuses first: Coach lights are typically protected by individual fuses in the 12V fuse panel — not circuit breakers. Check all low-voltage fuses. Fuse panel is often near the converter or in a basement compartment.
3
Individual light out: Check bulb first. Coach lights commonly use 1156, 1157, or wedge base bulbs. LED upgrades are direct drop-in replacements and dramatically reduce current draw.
4
Moisture intrusion: Coach light housings are notorious leak points. Remove the light fixture, inspect the mounting seal and caulk around all edges. Apply non-sag Dicor or butyl tape under the base before reinstalling.
5
Corrosion at socket: Exterior light sockets corrode from moisture exposure. Clean contacts with electrical contact cleaner. Dielectric grease on bulb base prevents future corrosion.
6
Running lights on the roof edge: Marker lights are notorious leak sources. If a light is out — pull the lens, check the bulb, but also inspect the mounting seal around the entire light base for water intrusion into the sidewall.
✓ Pro tip: LED retrofit bulbs for coach lights are a great upsell — lower draw extends battery life, much longer lifespan, and brighter output. Easy sell to customers who camp without hookups.

Manual Awning — Operation, Adjustment and Repair
1
Tension adjustment: Manual awnings have a tension knob on each end. If the fabric sags in the middle or flaps in wind — increase tension. If the awning is hard to crank — decrease tension. Adjust both ends equally.
2
Won't retract fully: Usually caused by debris in the roller tube or a bent support arm. Check the roller tube ends for blockage and inspect arm alignment. Support arms must be perfectly parallel for smooth retraction.
3
Fabric replacement: Measure roller tube length accurately before ordering. Most fabrics are sold by length and width. Installation requires rolling the new fabric tightly onto the tube — use a heat gun slightly to help tension.
4
Rafter arm adjustment: A-style awnings have adjustable rafter pitch arms. Ensure both sides are set at the same angle for proper water runoff. Water should shed away from the RV — never pool toward it.
5
Travel latch: Always verify the travel latch engages properly after retracting. A latch that doesn't click can allow the awning to deploy at highway speed — catastrophic damage results.
⚠ Never leave a manual awning extended in rain or wind without the stabilizer straps deployed. Pooled water weight and wind load can snap support arms and tear mounting hardware from the sidewall.

Electric Awning — Diagnosis and Repair
1
Won't extend or retract: Check 12V fuse panel first — electric awnings draw significant current and often have a dedicated fuse. Next check battery voltage — awning motor requires minimum 10.5V to operate.
2
Lippert/Carefree systems: Most common brands. Check for error codes on the wall switch display. Beep patterns indicate specific faults — consult the brand-specific manual for fault codes.
3
Motor test: Apply 12V directly to the motor leads to determine if the motor is functional or if the fault is in the control board or switch wiring. Motors are expensive — verify before replacing.
4
Manual override: All electric awnings have a manual override for emergencies. Usually a hex bolt on the roller tube end. Always show customers the override location — a stranded awning in bad weather is a real emergency.
5
Wind sensor: Many newer awnings have automatic wind retraction sensors. If the awning keeps retracting on its own in calm conditions — the wind sensor may be faulty or the sensitivity needs adjustment.
6
Common wiring failure: The wire harness runs through the awning arm and flexes every time the awning moves. Wire fatigue at flex points is very common — inspect harness closely for breaks especially at arm joints.
💡 Lippert OneControl app can diagnose many Lippert awning issues wirelessly. If the customer has a Lippert system it is worth checking app connectivity before doing hands-on diagnosis.

Outdoor Sink — Repair and Maintenance
1
No water flow: Check that the outdoor sink shutoff valve (usually in the wet bay or storage compartment) is open. Many owners forget this valve exists after winterization.
2
Faucet drips or leaks: Outdoor faucets use standard plumbing cartridges or packing — same repair as indoor. Shut off water supply, disassemble the faucet head, replace O-rings or cartridge. SharkBite fittings make line repairs easy in tight spaces.
3
Drain issues: Outdoor sink drains directly to gray tank or drains onto the ground depending on the unit. If draining to ground — verify this is allowed at the campsite. If gray tank — check for air lock in drain line.
4
Freeze damage: Outdoor sinks are highly vulnerable to freeze damage in Maine winters. If customer reports no flow after first freeze — inspect all exposed lines and faucet body for cracks before turning water back on.
5
Cabinet door seals: The cabinet enclosing the outdoor sink is a common source of water damage and rodent entry. Inspect door seals and corner caulk annually — replace foam weatherstripping if compressed or missing.

Outdoor Grill Connection — Maintenance and Repair
1
Quick connect fittings: Most RV outdoor grills use a proprietary quick-disconnect on the exterior wall. These fittings use a spring-loaded check valve that can stick or fail — perform bubble test annually.
2
Weak flame or won't light: Check the quick-connect fitting is fully engaged. Partially seated connectors restrict flow. Also verify the main propane valve is fully open and the excess flow valve hasn't tripped.
3
Leak at connection: If bubble test shows leak at the quick-connect fitting — the check valve O-ring is failing. The fitting can be disassembled and O-ring replaced or the entire fitting can be replaced. Use only propane-rated thread sealant on fittings.
4
Grill hose inspection: The hose from the quick-connect to the grill is often overlooked. Inspect for cracks, brittleness, and UV damage annually. Replace every 5 years or immediately if any damage is found.
💡 Quick-connect fittings are not universal — male and female connectors must match. Measure thread size and check if the fitting is 1/4" inverted flare or POL before ordering replacements.

Leveling Jack System — Types and Diagnosis
1
Key difference: Leveling jacks actually lift the RV to level it. Stabilizer jacks only stabilize — they are NOT designed to lift the coach weight. Never confuse the two.
2
Hydraulic systems (HWH, Power Gear, Lippert): Check hydraulic fluid level first — should be red/pink and clear. Milky or dark fluid indicates water contamination — change fluid. Low fluid = slow extension or failure to hold position.
3
Electric over hydraulic: Most common failure is corrosion at electrical connections. Start diagnosis at the power source and trace voltage to each relay and motor. Most failures are bad grounds or corroded connections — not failed motors.
4
Jacks won't retract: Hydraulic override procedure — locate manual release valve(s), open valve, manually push jacks up. Leave release valves open until repaired. Never drive with jacks extended.
5
Air in hydraulic lines: Causes uneven extension and poor holding. Bleed the system by cycling jacks fully up and down several times. Some systems require a specific bleeding procedure per the manufacturer manual.
6
Control module failures: Error codes on the display panel pinpoint faults. Lippert, HWH, and Power Gear all have specific error code lists. Always look up the exact code before replacing components — the most common fix is a dirty or loose connector, not a bad module.
⛔ Minimum battery voltage for reliable leveling jack operation is 10.5–11V under load. Low battery is the #1 cause of jack malfunctions and is often misdiagnosed as a jack failure. Check battery first always.

Manual Stabilizer Jacks — Maintenance and Repair
1
Remember: Stabilizer jacks do NOT level the RV — they only reduce rocking after the unit is already level. Extend them until snug contact with ground, not to lift weight.
2
Scissor jacks: Most common on travel trailers. Inspect the screw mechanism and pivot points for rust and damage. Lubricate the screw with white lithium grease. Check for stripped threads — stripped scissor jacks must be replaced.
3
A-frame jacks: Common on 5th wheels. Inspect the pin and hole mechanism for wear. Bent or cracked arms must be replaced — do not use damaged jacks.
4
Jack pads: Always use jack pads under stabilizer feet on soft ground. The foot digs in otherwise and provides no stability. Carry a set of X-Chock wheel chocks and stabilizer pads as standard equipment.
5
Rust treatment: Surface rust on stabilizer arms is cosmetic — treat with rust converter and repaint. Structural rust that has eaten through the metal wall thickness means replacement.
✓ Upgrading from stock scissor jacks to heavy-duty bolt-on stabilizers is a great upsell — dramatically reduces rocking and is a one-time installation customers appreciate every trip.

Electric Stabilizer Jacks — Diagnosis and Repair
1
Common brands: Lippert Ground Control, Stromberg Carlson, BAL/Noco, Power Gear. Each has specific troubleshooting procedures — identify brand before diagnosing.
2
Won't move: Check 12V fuse panel for blown fuse first. Electric stabilizers draw 15–20 amps per motor — fuses are common failure point. Also check battery voltage — below 10.5V motors won't operate properly.
3
One jack works, others don't: Usually a wiring or motor issue on the non-working jack(s). Test voltage at each motor connector. Check ground connections at each motor — ground failure is the most common cause.
4
Motor replacement: Motors are interchangeable across most brands with similar specs. Measure shaft diameter and motor bolt pattern before ordering. Many Lippert motors are cross-compatible.
5
Manual override: All electric stabilizers have a manual crank override — typically a 3/8" or 1/2" hex drive on the motor end. Always test and demonstrate this to customers — they need to know it for emergencies.

Manual Entry Steps — Repair and Maintenance
1
Folding step mechanism: Inspect pivot points and fold mechanism for bent components, rust, and missing hardware. Steps that don't fold fully can be damaged on the road or prevent door closure.
2
Step wobble: Usually caused by worn bushings at pivot points or loose mounting hardware. Check all mounting bolts at the frame attachment point — these loosen from road vibration.
3
Step surface: Inspect step treads for cracks, worn grip surface, and corrosion. Replace worn non-slip pads — a slippery wet step is a serious liability issue especially for elderly customers.
4
Lubrication: Apply white lithium grease or silicone lubricant to all pivot points and the fold mechanism. Steps should fold and unfold smoothly — stiff steps indicate dry pivot points.
5
Step replacement: Most manual steps are bolted on with 4 mounting bolts to the frame rail or door frame. Measure step width and depth before ordering — widths vary from 18" to 24" commonly.

Electric Entry Steps — Diagnosis and Repair
1
Common brands: Kwikee (now Lippert), Stromberg Carlson, Venture. Kwikee is the most common — uses a 12V DC motor with a gear-drive mechanism.
2
Steps won't extend: Check fuse first. Kwikee steps have a dedicated in-line fuse near the step motor — commonly 15 or 20A. Also check door switch connection — steps often extend/retract when door opens/closes.
3
Step control switch: Most electric steps have a manual override switch on the step control module. If door switch fails the override switch allows manual operation. Locate this switch and test it during diagnosis.
4
Motor test: Apply 12V directly to motor leads to test motor independently. If motor runs when direct powered but not through control module — the module or wiring is the issue, not the motor.
5

Low Point Drains — Operation and Repair
1
What they are: Low point drains are the lowest points in the hot and cold water lines — plus one for the fresh tank in many units. Gravity drain as much water as possible during winterization to prevent freeze damage.
2
Typical location: Usually accessed from the underbelly or a storage compartment. Often colored red (hot) and blue (cold). Most have a pull cap or valve handle to open.

Tank Water Heater (Suburban / Atwood-Dometic)
1
Know your brand: Suburban uses a magnesium anode rod — inspect every 6–12 months, replace when over 50–75% depleted. Atwood/Dometic uses an aluminum tank — NO anode rod. Never install a magnesium anode in an Atwood tank.

1
How they work: Tankless units ignite when minimum water flow is detected (typically 0.5–1.0 GPM). They require consistent water flow to maintain ignition. Partially closed valves, clogged aerators, or low-flow showerheads cause cold water bursts and short cycling.

RV Stove/Oven — Diagnosis and Repair
1
Normal operation sequence: RV ovens have a manual pilot that must be lit first. Press and hold knob to supply LP to pilot, hold flame to thermocouple for 30 seconds until it stays lit. Then turn to desired temp — thermostat regulates flow to main burner. If pilot goes out, safety magnet drops and shuts off LP.
2
Pilot won't stay lit: Thermocouple is most common cause. Hold the pilot longer — thermocouple needs 30–45 seconds of heat to generate enough millivoltage to hold the safety valve open. If it still drops out — test thermocouple with multimeter or replace it.
3
Oven won't heat to temperature: Could be thermostat failure, clogged orifice, or weak regulator pressure. Check LP pressure first (should be 11" WC). Inspect oven temperature bulb in rear of oven — if kinked or damaged it gives false readings to the thermostat.
4
Internal LP regulator: Most RV stoves have a built-in secondary regulator under the cooktop that reduces from 11" WC to 10" WC. This regulator can fail — if all burners are weak but system LP pressure is fine, this internal regulator is the culprit.
5
LP detector lockout: On motorhomes the LP detector (located near floor level) must be ON for LP to flow to appliances. If the LP detector is off or has tripped — no gas reaches any appliance including the stove. Check detector first if all LP appliances fail simultaneously.
⚠ Stove burners have NO thermocouple — LP flows freely if the knob is open with no flame. Never leave a knob open without flame present. Always check for gas smell before lighting.

Cooktop Igniter and Burner Repair
1
Igniter won't spark: Check the battery in the igniter button first — many cooktops use a AA or AAA battery that customers overlook. Also inspect the ceramic insulator on the igniter tip for cracks — a crack sends the spark through the insulator instead of to the burner.
2
Spark gap spec: The gap between the igniter electrode and the burner must be 3/16" for reliable ignition. Too wide or too narrow and it won't light. Adjust electrode position carefully — this solves many "igniter not working" calls without replacing any parts.
3
Spark module failure: If no burners spark, the spark ignition module on the back panel has likely failed. Label all wires before removing — modules have multiple wire positions that must go back exactly. Replacement modules are inexpensive and widely available.
4
Wire shorting on gas tube: A common but overlooked cause — the igniter wire's insulation melts from heat and contacts the gas supply tube, shorting the spark to ground instead of the burner. Lift cooktop top (push back and lift), inspect wire routing. Reroute and use high-temp wire loom if needed.
5
Burner ports clogged: Spider webs and debris in burner ports are extremely common after storage. Use a toothpick or fine wire to clear ports — never use a drill bit as it enlarges the orifice. Clean with warm soapy water, rinse thoroughly, dry completely before use.
💡 If the burner lights manually with a match but igniter doesn't spark — it is purely an igniter issue. This simple test saves time by ruling out LP supply problems immediately.

RV Refrigerator — How It Works and Diagnosis
1
How absorption fridges work: No compressor — heat from propane flame or 120V element causes ammonia solution to percolate through tubes, producing cooling. Completely different from home fridges. This is why the RV must be reasonably level — gravity drives circulation.
2
Level requirement: Must be within 3 degrees side-to-side and 6 degrees front-to-back for proper cooling. Running unlevel for extended time can permanently damage the cooling unit. If fridge was run severely unlevel, try the rubber mallet trick — tap cooling unit tubes gently to dislodge sediment blockage, run for several hours.
3
Not cooling on either mode: Check thermistor first — disconnect the thermistor wire from control board. If fridge starts cooling — thermistor has failed. At room temp: ~2000 ohms. In ice water: ~9500 ohms. Outside spec = replace thermistor.
4
Not cooling on LP only: Check manual gas valve is open (Dometic and Norcold both have one). Inspect burner orifice for spider webs — one spider web is enough to block ignition. Clean flue tube. Check LP pressure at system (must be 11" WC).
5
Not cooling on 120V only: Test 120V heating element — check for continuity. Inspect control board fuse. Check that shore power is connected and breaker is on. Verify 120V at the fridge receptacle with multimeter.
6
Condenser plugged: Remove exterior access panel. If condenser fins are packed with insulation debris — clean thoroughly. This is a very common and very simple fix that looks like a major problem from inside the RV.
7
Yellow ammonia residue: Oily yellowish residue on cooling unit coils = ammonia leak. Cooling unit has failed — replacement or new fridge required. Cannot be field repaired.
⛔ Ammonia leaks from cooling units are a safety hazard — strong odor inside RV. Ventilate immediately. Cooling unit replacement requires a qualified technician.

RV Furnace — Normal Operation and Diagnosis
1
Normal sequence: Thermostat calls for heat → blower fan starts (cold air for 15–30 sec) → sail switch closes confirming airflow → igniter sparks and gas valve opens → burner lights → hot air within seconds. Fan runs 45–90 seconds after shutdown to cool heat exchanger.
2
Furnace won't start at all: Check 12V battery voltage first — furnace needs strong 12V to operate. Low battery is #1 cause of furnace failure misdiagnosed as furnace failure. Check 12V fuse panel for blown furnace fuse. Verify thermostat is set to HEAT and setpoint is above room temp.
3
Fan runs but no ignition: Purge air from LP lines first — run stove burner 10 seconds to fill line with gas, then retry furnace. If still no ignition — check exterior exhaust vent for spider webs or mud dauber nests. One bug or nest can completely block ignition.
4
Sail switch: Critical component — a small flap inside the furnace housing that confirms adequate airflow before allowing ignition. Dirty return air path (under fridge, near floor) can restrict airflow and prevent sail switch from closing. Vacuum the return air area monthly during heating season.
5
Ignition lockout: After 3 failed ignition attempts the furnace locks out for 1 hour. Reset by switching thermostat off and back on. Find the root cause before the customer resets it repeatedly — repeated lockouts stress the control board.
6
Flame sensor / igniter: The igniter is also the flame sensor on most RV furnaces. If flame lights but shuts off after 6–10 seconds — flame sensor is not reading the flame. Clean with fine emery cloth. A cracked ceramic insulator allows spark to short — replace igniter.
7
Control board LED codes: Most Suburban and Atwood/Dometic boards flash diagnostic codes on the control board LEDs. Count the flashes to look up the fault code in the brand manual before replacing parts.
⛔ Never override a limit switch or sail switch — these are safety devices. A furnace that bypasses its safety controls can ignite LP gas or overheat the heat exchanger causing a fire.

RV Air Conditioner — Diagnosis and Repair
1
Not cooling — first checks: Clean or replace filters — dirty filters are the #1 cause of poor cooling. Filters should be vacuumed every 2 weeks in heavy use. Inspect both interior evaporator coils and exterior condenser coils for debris. Clean condenser coils on roof with soft brush — bent fins reduce airflow drastically.
2
Compressor not coming on: Check shore power voltage — below 110V AC and the compressor won't start or will quickly trip. Check capacitors with multimeter — a swollen, leaking, or open capacitor is very common and inexpensive to replace. Test compressor and run capacitor.
3
Temperature drop test: Working AC should drop air temperature 15–20°F from return air to supply air. If return is 85°F, supply should be 65–70°F. Less than 15°F drop with clean filters and coils = possible low refrigerant or failing compressor.
4
Fan runs but compressor doesn't hum: Start capacitor failure is very common in RV units — capacitor gives the compressor the extra kick to start. Test with multimeter capacitance setting. Replacement is inexpensive — typically $15–$30.
5
Water leaking inside: Clogged condensate drain on roof unit — remove shroud, clear drain holes. Also check gasket seal between roof unit and interior ceiling assembly — a failing gasket allows condensate to drip inside.
6
Short cycling (turns on and off rapidly): Iced evaporator coils — caused by dirty filters or low refrigerant. Allow to thaw with fan only mode, then address root cause. Also check thermostat placement — too close to supply vent gives false readings.
⚠ RV AC refrigerant is a sealed system — recharging requires EPA 608 certification and specialized equipment. If refrigerant leak is suspected, this requires a certified technician. Do not attempt to add refrigerant without proper certification.

Fan won't run: Bathroom vent fans run on 12V DC. Check 12V fuse panel for a blown vent fan fuse. Check that the switch wiring connections at the fan are secure — vibration loosens crimp connectors in RV vent fans frequently.
2
Motor replacement: Most RV bathroom fans use a standard 12V brushless motor that is replaceable without replacing the entire unit. Common replacement motors are available from Ventline, Dometic, and Fan-Tastic — measure the motor diameter and voltage before ordering.
3
Fan makes noise but weak airflow: Debris in the fan blade — leaves, insects, and dust accumulate on fan blades reducing airflow. Remove roof vent cover and clean fan blade. Also check that the damper flap opens freely — a stuck damper severely restricts airflow.
4
Vent cover cracks: Plastic vent covers crack from UV exposure after several years. A cracked cover is a water intrusion point. Replacement covers are inexpensive and install with 4 screws — reseal with self-leveling Dicor around the base after installation.
5
Lid won't open or close: The lid mechanism uses a small gear or cam and spring. These crack from UV exposure. Replacement kits for most brands are available. Ventline and Dometic are the most common RV bathroom vent brands — identify the brand from the unit label before ordering parts.

RV Toilet — Repair and Maintenance
1
Common brands: Thetford, Dometic (formerly SeaLand), and Sealand are the most common. Know the brand before ordering parts — seal kits and valve kits are brand and model specific.
2
Sewer odors in coach: The #1 cause is a dried or damaged floor seal (also called a base seal or closet seal). This rubber seal sits between the toilet base and the floor connection to the black tank. When it dries out or cracks — sewer gas enters freely. Replace the floor seal every 2–3 years as preventive maintenance.
3
Toilet won't hold water in bowl: Blade valve seal failure — the rubber seal around the toilet's ball or blade valve is worn. Thetford and Dometic both sell seal/valve rebuild kits for around $15–$25. Drain the system, remove toilet, replace seal kit.
4
Water constantly running into bowl: Water valve on the toilet is not seating properly — debris or wear preventing the valve from closing fully. Disassemble the water valve, clean or replace the seal. Thetford part #34120 fits many models.
5
Pump cycling intermittently with no faucets running: The toilet water valve is the most common hidden leak causing the pump to cycle when all faucets are off. Press the pedal slightly — if water seeps into the bowl — the valve seal has failed.
💡 Toilet seal and valve kits are an excellent part to carry in stock on the truck. They are inexpensive, small, and toilet seal failure is one of the most common odor complaints you will get on any service call.

Toilet Water Supply Lines — Diagnosis and Repair
1
Supply line leak: The water line connecting to the toilet is either a flexible braided line or a PEX line with a push-fit or compression fitting. Inspect the connection at the toilet valve and at the wall fitting for drips. Hand tighten first — many leaks stop with simple tightening.
2
Shutoff valve before toilet: Many RV toilets have a shutoff valve on the supply line — often a 1/4 turn ball valve. If valve is partially closed it restricts flow. Ensure it is fully open. If the valve leaks at the stem or body — replace it with a 1/2" PEX ball valve.
3
Freeze damage: Supply lines in the bathroom that run through unheated spaces are vulnerable to freeze damage in Maine winters. After first freeze — inspect the entire supply line run from pump to toilet before pressurizing the system. Even a pinhole crack floods quickly under pump pressure.
4
PEX line repair: Most RV plumbing uses PEX tubing. SharkBite push-to-connect fittings allow fast, reliable repairs without special tools in tight spaces. Cut out the damaged section cleanly with a pipe cutter, deburr, push in SharkBite fittings and coupling.
5
Water hammer at toilet flush: Rapid valve operation causes pressure spike — water hammer noise. An accumulator tank on the system reduces water hammer significantly. Also check that all supply line support clips are in place and lines are not vibrating against cabinet walls.

RV Water Pump — Diagnosis and Repair
1
Won't run: Check 12V fuse for pump in fuse panel first. Verify battery voltage is above 11.5V. Check pump switch is on. Verify winterizing selector valve is in the TANK position — not winterize. This catches a significant number of "pump not working" calls after spring startup.
2
Pump runs but no water: Tank may be empty — verify water level. Strainer on pump inlet may be clogged — clean strainer screen. Pump may need priming — open a faucet, turn pump on, let it run until water flows. Check that no air leak exists on the inlet side.
3
Pump cycles when no faucets are open: There is a leak somewhere in the system. Isolate by capping the pump outlet directly — if it still cycles, the leak is internal to the pump. If cycling stops — the leak is downstream. Toilet valve and water heater T&P valve are the most common hidden leak sources.
4
Pulsing water flow: Caused by air in the lines, a failing pump diaphragm, or the absence of an accumulator tank. Check all fittings for air intrusion. If diaphragm is worn — rebuild kit or replacement pump needed. Installing an accumulator tank is the best long-term fix for minor pulsing.
5
Noisy pump vibration: Factory-installed pumps are often mounted directly to the floor without isolation. Remove pump, add rubber or felt isolation pad under the pump body and between mounting screws and pump. This eliminates most pump noise complaints without replacing the pump.
6
Pump replacement: Most RV pumps are 12V diaphragm pumps (Shurflo, Flojet, Aquajet). Match the GPM and PSI rating of the original. Standard connections are 1/2" barb fittings. Use Teflon tape on all threaded connections, hose clamps on all barb connections.
✓ Carrying a replacement Shurflo 4008 (3.0 GPM, 55 PSI) or equivalent on the truck covers the majority of standard RV pump replacements. It is the most common replacement pump in the industry.

Battery Types — Know What You Are Working With
1
Flooded lead-acid (FLA): Most common on older units. Requires distilled water top-up every 30 days. Check water level at fill caps — should touch bottom of fill well, not overfilled. Produces hydrogen gas during charging — requires ventilation. Most affordable but most maintenance.
2
AGM (Absorbed Glass Mat): Sealed, maintenance-free, no water to add. Handles vibration better than FLA. More expensive but widely used on newer units. Requires an AGM-compatible charger/converter — standard converter settings can damage AGM over time.
3
LiFePO4 (Lithium): Increasingly common on new units and upgrades. No maintenance, much lighter, vastly more usable capacity. Requires a lithium-compatible charger — standard converters may not charge properly. Cannot be charged below 32°F without a self-heating model.
4
6V vs 12V wiring: Two 6V batteries wired in series (+ to −) = 12V system with doubled capacity. Two 12V batteries wired in parallel (+ to +, − to −) = 12V system with doubled capacity. Always draw from opposite ends of a battery bank for balanced discharge.

Battery Diagnosis and Maintenance
1
Load test: Voltage alone doesn't tell the full story — a battery can show 12.6V but fail under load. Most auto parts stores load test for free. For deep cycle batteries, a dedicated deep cycle load tester gives more accurate results than a standard automotive tester.
2
Terminal corrosion: White or blue-green buildup on terminals causes resistance and voltage drop. Clean with baking soda and water solution, rinse, dry, apply dielectric grease or terminal protector spray after cleaning.
3
Battery disconnect switch: If 12V items don't work but battery is good — check that the battery disconnect switch is in the ON position. This is the #1 overlooked item on spring startup calls and after storage.
4
Parasitic drain test: If battery drains overnight with nothing running — connect a multimeter in series with the negative battery cable (current mode). More than 50mA draw with everything off indicates a parasitic drain. Pull fuses one at a time until draw drops to find the circuit.
5
Storage: Flooded and AGM batteries self-discharge at 3–5% per month. Use a smart battery maintainer during storage. LiFePO4 self-discharges less than 2% per month and can be stored at 50% SOC for long periods without harm.

Interior Coach Lights — Diagnosis and Repair
1
All lights out: Check 12V battery voltage and disconnect switch. Check 12V fuse panel — interior lighting usually has one or more dedicated fuses. If on shore power and lights still out — check converter output voltage at battery terminals (should be 13.2–14.4V).
2
Dim or flickering lights: Usually indicates low battery voltage or a failing converter. If lights are bright when plugged in but dim off-grid — battery is weak. If dim even when plugged in — converter output is low. Test converter output at battery terminals with multimeter.
3
Individual light out: Check the bulb first — incandescent and fluorescent bulbs fail regularly. LED replacements are direct drop-in for most RV fixtures and last dramatically longer. Check for 12V at the fixture with multimeter if bulb checks out good.
4
LED upgrade: Most RV interior fixtures use standard automotive bulb bases (1141, 1156, 194, etc.) or G4 pins. LED retrofits are available for all common types. Draw is 80–90% less than incandescent — a worthwhile upgrade for any customer doing regular boondocking.
5
Switch failure: RV wall switches are typically 12V DC toggle switches. Test for voltage on both sides of the switch with multimeter. If voltage on the input side but not the output when switched on — switch has failed. Standard 12V automotive switches are direct replacements.
💡 Wire colors in RV lighting are NOT always consistent between manufacturers. Always test with a multimeter before assuming which wire is positive and which is negative. Using a test light on 12V circuits is fast and reliable.

Converter — What It Does and How to Test It
1
What it does: Converts 120V AC shore power to 12V DC. Powers all 12V systems when plugged in AND charges the coach batteries. Without a working converter — 12V systems only run until the battery is depleted even when plugged into shore power.
2
Common brands: WFCO, Progressive Dynamics (PD), Parallax, Magnetek/Newmar. Most modern units are combination converter/chargers with 3 or 4 stage charging. Older units may be single-stage which can overcharge batteries left connected long-term.
3
Testing output: With RV plugged into shore power — measure voltage at battery terminals with multimeter. Should read 13.2–14.4V. If still reading battery resting voltage (12.6V or lower) — converter is not charging. Check AC breaker for converter and the large reverse-polarity DC fuse (usually 30–40A) on the converter itself first.
4
Converter fan running constantly or loudly: Normal for converter to have a cooling fan but a fan running continuously at high speed means converter is working hard. Check for excessive current draw, poor ventilation, or a battery that will not accept a charge (bad cell drawing high current).
5
Replacement: WFCO 8900 series and Progressive Dynamics PD4000 series are the most common direct replacements. Match amperage output to original (45A, 55A, 75A are common sizes). Larger converters charge faster and handle more simultaneous 12V loads.
⚠ A weak or failing battery can mimic converter failure — battery draws excessive current trying to recover and causes low output voltage. Always verify battery health before condemning the converter.

Inverter — Diagnosis and Troubleshooting
1
What it does: The opposite of a converter — converts 12V DC battery power into 120V AC. Allows running AC appliances (microwave, TV, outlets) when NOT connected to shore power. Draws heavily from batteries — a 1000W inverter draws about 90A from 12V batteries.
2
Inverter/charger combo: Many motorhomes have a combined inverter/charger (Xantrex, Magnum, Victron). This single unit acts as both inverter (off-grid) and battery charger (on shore power). Failure affects both functions simultaneously.
3
No output when on battery: Check battery voltage first — inverters have a low-voltage cutoff (typically 10.5–11V) to protect batteries. Below cutoff the inverter shuts down. Charge batteries first. Also check the large DC fuse or breaker between batteries and inverter — these are high-amperage (typically 100–400A ANL fuse).
4
Error codes and fault lights: Most inverters display fault codes or flash error patterns. Consult the brand-specific manual before replacing components. Common faults: overload (too much load), overtemp (needs cooling), low battery, or internal fault.
5
Overload trips: High-draw appliances — hair dryers, electric heaters, microwaves, AC units — can exceed the inverter's rated wattage and trip overload protection. Check the inverter's watt rating against what is plugged in. A 1000W inverter cannot run a 1500W hair dryer.
⛔ Never work on inverter wiring without disconnecting from shore power AND the battery bank. Inverters store charge in capacitors that remain dangerous even after power is removed. If you smell burning or see smoke — disconnect immediately and do not reconnect until professionally inspected.

Interior 120V Receptacles — Diagnosis
1
No power at outlet — first steps: Check the AC breaker panel — a tripped breaker may look fine but be in the middle position. Flip all breakers fully OFF then back ON to reset. Then check all GFCI outlets — interior outlets are often downstream of a GFCI in the bathroom or kitchen.
2
Test with multimeter: Should read approximately 120V AC between hot and neutral. If zero voltage — check breaker and GFCI. If reading 60V — could be a floating neutral or open neutral — this requires professional diagnosis as it can damage appliances.
3
Outlet tester: A $7 outlet tester (3-light type) identifies wiring faults instantly — open ground, reversed polarity, open hot, open neutral. Essential tool to carry on every electrical service call. Plug it in and read the light pattern.
4
Replacing an outlet: Disconnect from shore power before opening any outlet box. Most RV outlets use standard 15A or 20A residential outlets. Pigtail connections are common in RV outlet boxes — one outlet can feed the next downstream. Identify all wires and document before disconnecting.
5
Outlet warm to the touch: A warm outlet indicates a loose connection arcing internally — serious fire hazard. Disconnect shore power immediately, open the outlet box, identify and repair the loose connection. Never ignore a warm outlet.
⚠ Always disconnect from shore power AND verify no power with a multimeter or non-contact voltage tester before opening any outlet or switch box. 120V AC can kill. Verify twice before touching wires.

GFCI Receptacles — Diagnosis and Replacement
1
What GFCI protects: Detects current imbalance of as little as 5mA between hot and neutral — indicating current is flowing through a person or to ground. Trips in about 1/40 of a second. Required in bathrooms, kitchens, exterior locations, and any outlet near water.
2
Daisy-chain effect: One GFCI outlet can protect multiple downstream outlets on the same circuit. If any outlet in the RV loses power — finding and resetting the GFCI that feeds it is always the first step. Map your GFCI circuits by tripping each one and noting which outlets lose power.
3
GFCI keeps tripping: Most common cause is moisture — inside the outlet box itself from rain or condensation. Remove the cover plate and allow to dry completely, inspect for signs of water. If moisture is present on an exterior outlet — replace the weatherproof cover gasket.
4
Converter and water heater elements: The converter and electric water heater element are common sources of current leakage that trip campground GFCI pedestals. To isolate — trip the breakers for those items inside the RV one at a time while plugged in. When the pedestal GFCI stops tripping — you found the leaky appliance.
5
GFCI replacement: Use a weatherproof-rated GFCI for any exterior or bathroom location. Must be rated 15A or 20A to match the circuit. Always connect the LINE side (from breaker) and LOAD side (to downstream outlets) correctly — reversed connection defeats the protection on downstream outlets.
💡 GFCI outlets have a finite lifespan — typically 10–15 years. A GFCI that tests OK with the test button but won't reset or trips for no apparent reason is simply at end of life. Replacement is inexpensive and fast.

Smoke Detectors — Maintenance and Replacement
1
Power source: RV smoke detectors are almost always battery-powered (9V) — not hardwired to 12V like LP and CO detectors. Check and replace the battery annually or when the unit chirps a low battery warning.
2
Lifespan: Smoke detectors have an expiration date printed on the unit — typically 8–10 years from manufacture. Replace the entire unit at expiration regardless of whether it appears to function. The sensor degrades over time.
3
False alarms from cooking: Smoke detectors in RV kitchens are frequently triggered by cooking steam and smoke — the kitchen is extremely close to the detector. This is a normal RV experience. Covering the detector while cooking (with a bag or cover) is common but advise customers to uncover immediately after.
4
Testing: Push the test button monthly. The test button only tests the electronics — not the sensor's ability to detect smoke. Dedicated smoke aerosol spray (available at hardware stores) tests the actual sensor. Test with aerosol annually.
5
Cleaning: Dust accumulation inside the detection chamber causes false alarms. Vacuum the detector vents gently with a soft brush attachment annually. Do not use aerosol sprays (hairspray, cooking spray, cleaners) near smoke detectors.

Carbon Monoxide (CO) Detector — Critical Safety Information
1
Why CO is deadly: Colorless, odorless gas from incomplete combustion of propane, gasoline, and diesel. Produced by the furnace, water heater, generator, and any nearby idling engines. Displaces oxygen in the bloodstream — symptoms (headache, dizziness, nausea) mimic the flu. Victims often die in their sleep.
2
Power source: RV CO detectors are hardwired to the 12V DC system — not battery powered. They remain active even with the battery disconnect on because they are usually wired directly to the battery. Disconnecting the battery disables the detector — a critical safety issue during storage.
3
Placement: CO is slightly lighter than air and disperses evenly — mount at breathing height (4–5 feet from floor) near sleeping areas. Combination CO/LP units should be mounted low (LP sinks) — follow manufacturer's placement instructions.
4
Lifespan: CO sensor degrades over time — typically 5–7 years for combination units. End-of-life units enter a warning mode (intermittent chirp every 30–60 seconds) that cannot be stopped — the unit must be replaced. Check manufacture date on the back of every unit you inspect.
5
RVs built before 2005: Not required to have a CO detector from the factory. Any pre-2005 RV that does not have a CO detector should have one installed immediately — recommend this on every inspection.
⛔ If CO alarm sounds: Get everyone out immediately. Do not stop to investigate. Call 911. Do not re-enter until cleared by emergency services. CO poisoning can incapacitate before the victim realizes the danger.

LP Gas Detector — Diagnosis and Replacement
1
Placement — critical difference from CO: Propane is heavier than air — it sinks to the floor. LP detectors must be mounted within 12–18 inches of the floor. Never mount LP detectors near the ceiling — they will not detect a propane leak until the room is heavily saturated.
2
Power: Hardwired to 12V DC — typically stays live even through the battery disconnect switch. This is intentional — the LP detector must function any time propane is present in the RV, including when the battery disconnect is engaged.
3
End-of-life warning vs active leak: EOL warning = slow intermittent chirp every 30–60 seconds, often with a specific LED flash pattern. Active leak = loud continuous alarm. Distinguish these carefully — an EOL warning in the middle of the night alarms customers but is NOT a gas leak emergency. Check manufacture date first.
4
Lifespan: 5–7 years from manufacture date. The date is printed on the back or side of the unit. An expired unit must be replaced — the sensor loses accuracy and may fail to detect an actual leak.
5
False alarms from cleaning products: Many household cleaners, aerosol sprays, and even some cooking oils trigger LP detectors. If customer reports random false alarms — ask about cleaning products used recently. Thoroughly ventilate the RV before concluding the detector is faulty.
6
Replacement: Safe-T-Alert (MTI Industries) and RV Safe are the two major brands. Match the footprint of the old unit or use a trim ring to cover the gap. Always buy a 12V DC hardwired unit — never a battery-powered or 120V household unit for RV use.
💡 Recommending a combination LP/CO detector (Safe-T-Alert 35 series or equivalent) is the professional choice — covers both hazards in one unit, one installation, one wire connection. An excellent and inexpensive upsell on any service call.

Shore Power Cord — Inspection and Repair
1
30A vs 50A: 30A cord has 3 prongs (2 hot + 1 neutral). 50A cord has 4 prongs (2 hot + 1 neutral + 1 ground). Never use a 50A RV on a 30A service without a proper 30A→50A adapter — running on 30A limits available power and you cannot run two AC units simultaneously. Opposite direction (50A on 30A service) requires a 50A→30A dogbone adapter.
2
Inspect before every use: Check both the male plug end (going to pedestal) and female receptacle end (going to RV) for bent or burnt prongs, melted plastic, cracked insulation, or signs of overheating. A damaged cord is a serious fire hazard — replace immediately.
3
Plug replacement: If only the plug end is damaged — you do not need to replace the entire cord. Cut off the damaged end. Buy a replacement 30A or 50A male plug. Follow the instructions included with the plug for proper wire length and connection sequence. Match wire gauge to the cord.
4
Hot or loose connection at pedestal: If the cord feels warm where it connects to the pedestal — the pedestal receptacle is worn and has poor contact. Report to campground management immediately — do not continue using. A loose or worn pedestal connection generates heat and can cause a fire.
5
Surge protector: Every RV should use a quality surge protector/EMS (electrical management system) between the pedestal and the RV cord. Campground power quality varies significantly — low voltage, reverse polarity, and power surges can damage the converter, appliances, and wiring. EMS devices disconnect power if conditions are unsafe.
✓ Pro tip: Surge protectors/EMS devices are an excellent upsell on every shore power call. Progressive Industries and Hughes Autoformers make the most respected units. A $150–$300 EMS protects thousands of dollars of RV electrical components.

RV Solar System — Components and Troubleshooting
1
System components: Solar panels → charge controller → battery bank → (optional) inverter. The charge controller is the brain — it manages charging current, prevents overcharge, and often displays system data. A faulty charge controller is the most common solar system failure.
2
No charging from solar: Check charge controller display first — most modern MPPT controllers have a display showing panel input voltage, battery voltage, and charge current. No panel input voltage = panel wiring issue or panels shaded/dirty. Input voltage present but no charge current = controller or battery issue.
3
Panel voltage check: Disconnect panels from controller. Measure open-circuit voltage (Voc) across the panel terminals in full sun — should be close to the rated Voc on the panel label (typically 18–22V for a 100W 12V panel). Significantly lower voltage = shading, dirty panel, or damaged panel.
4
MPPT vs PWM controllers: MPPT (Maximum Power Point Tracking) is the modern standard — more efficient, better for higher-voltage panel configurations. PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) is older and less efficient. They are NOT interchangeable — panel array voltage must match controller type.
5
Battery compatibility: Charge controller must be programmed for the battery type — flooded, AGM, or LiFePO4. Wrong charging profile damages batteries. If customer recently switched to lithium — verify the charge controller is set or capable of LiFePO4 charging profile.
6
Panel cleaning: A thin layer of dust, bird droppings, or pine pollen can reduce solar output by 20–30%. Clean panels with water and a soft cloth or sponge — no abrasives. In Maine's environment with pollen and tree debris, panels may need cleaning monthly during camping season.
